Bird Call Detection System

By: Pulkit Komal (pulkit.k@tatrasdata.com), Nupur Kapur (nupur.k@tatrasdata.com)

Birds are a significant element in our biosphere. The ecology of a forest, jungle or to some extent human habitats depends on the birds. Due to the continuous urban expansion over past few decades in India (not to mention other developing countries as well) massive deforestation is taking place. One evident consequence of this is an irreversible destruction of bird habitat. This leads to extinction of different species over a not too long period of time. This project is a part of bigger work that analyze this pattern around different places in Northern India and seek possible remedies of the problem through IoT and advanced AI based tools in the back-end.

The audio data that contain bird call can be recorded in different environments, one of them can be an enclosure for birds where we will get higher bird call presence in a certain interval. Also in this situation the ambient sound (non-bird call audios) will be much less. The second environment can be either a forest, jungle or similar natural environments or even human habitats like city, towns, villages etc. The audios that comes from the second environment may contain different non-bird audio regions, they are

1. Geophonic sounds like wind, leaves

2. Anthropogenic sounds like human voice

3. Other animal sounds that are present in same environment

4. Occasional sounds that are non-biological in nature, like machine/ vehicle sounds

Currently, the data for any such ecological learning is either paid or not readily available so the objective is to make our data (recorded via IOT devices) accessible to all data scientists across the globe and also to store, process, and analyze the audio data recorded or uploaded by practitioners/domain experts on our web portal. We aim to use cloud solutions for storage and data computation which will enable practitioners/domain experts to access the system across the globe. The modules we are going to develop will help experts to understand the ecological data better by providing visualizations and an automatic annotation tool that will provide not only birdcall-related annotation but other ecological annotations as well(car horn, children playing, etc..).

Feature Extraction

Feature extraction is an essential part of analysing and finding relations between different things. The models cannot understand the audio data directly so it is converted into an understandable format using feature extraction. During our model training we used MFCC (Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients) and Mel Spectrogram to extract features from the audio files.

  • In MFCC, we apply M triangular filters on a set of frequencies to select a few of them. The filter is applied to the audio. For each position, the values are summed up. If there are M filters, we will have M values for one position, and for T such positions we will get (T X M) shape array.
  • Mel spectrogram is a spectrogram that uses a Mel scale on the y axis. Mel scale is the perceived frequency. Human ears convert the measured frequency to a smaller value and that is known as perceived frequency.

Audio Denoising Pipeline

  • The audio data that contain bird call can be recorded in different environments that can be either a forest, jungle or similar natural environments or even human habitats like city, towns, villages etc The audios that comes from these environment interfere in bird species detection and hence, removal of this ambient noise is of the utmost importance.
  • The first step in audio denoising pipeline is to pass the audio through the MixIT model. MixIT model was introduced by Google which helped in sound separation. This model takes an audio as input and generates four individual tracks.
  • Our Urban classifier is run on these four tracks in order to predict bird calls and different types of noises. In order to clean the audio further, we select the one with the highest likelihood of having a bird call in it.
  • A pre-emphasis filter is further applied to remove some of the noise present in the recording
  • In some instances, there is still some background noise that can be heard, so we perform spectral subtraction on the audio file to remove it. This means creating a file with noise (usually the first 0.5 seconds are sufficient) and subtracting it from the main audio file. The cleaned audio file is then amplified by 50db before storing it in Amazon S3 bucket.

Mixit model

Google's MixIt AI uses machine learning to separate multiple voices or speakers from an audio recording. The technology can be applied to bird call audio recordings, allowing researchers to distinguish between different bird species and identify individual bird calls, even when multiple birds are singing in the same recording. This would help in monitoring bird populations and understanding changes in their behavior and habitats over time.

Pre-emphasis filter and Spectral Subtraction

Pre-emphasis filter is used to amplify higher frequencies. pre-emphasis filter is useful in several ways:

  1. Balance the frequency spectrum since high frequencies usually have smaller magnitudes compared to lower frequencies
  2. Avoid numerical problems during the Fourier transform operation and
  3. Improve the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).

Spectral Subtraction is another denoising technique in which denoising is done by subtracting the magnitudes of the signal and the noise part. This method uses only the magnitude of both the audio signal and the noisy part and subtract them and in the end add the phase (which is kept constant) to it. The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) is also calculated for the signal to detect how much noise is removed from the signal.

Urban Sound Classification

Urban sound classification is a field of study that involves using machine learning techniques to classify and recognize different sounds that occur in urban environments, such as car horns, sirens, construction noise, and music. One important aspect of this field is the ability to distinguish between different types of sounds, which can aid in noise pollution reduction and improve urban planning.

In recent years, researchers have also focused on classifying natural sounds that occur in urban environments, such as bird calls. Bird calls are an important component of urban soundscapes, and their identification and monitoring can provide valuable information about biodiversity and the impact of urbanization on ecosystems.

Machine learning algorithms can be trained to recognize different bird species based on their calls, which can aid in monitoring changes in bird populations and understanding the effects of urbanization on their habitats. Unsupervised Methods

Unsupervised learning is a technique which is used to find patterns within data. To understand our data better, 1000 audios with ambient noise were taken and mel spectrograms for each of them were extracted. After saving the mel spectrogram images, pre-trained model of VGG16 was used for feature extraction in which last two layers, that is, the output layer of the model was removed to obtain array of features. PCA was used after this for dimensionality reduction. The resultant array of images was then fed to KMeans algorithm, which gave three clusters. One cluster had all the bird calls, the second cluster had all the insect noise and the third cluster has ambient noise recordings. Similarly, other unsupervised learning algorithms like Agglomerative clustering, Birch, DBSCAN, and Spectral Bi-clustering were used to see which algorithm performed better. Almost all the algorithms performed equally but in Kmeans, the overlap of bird call cluster and ambient noise cluster were less in comparison to others.

Future Scope

The future scope of birdcall detection systems is quite promising. With the advancement of technology and machine learning, these systems can become more accurate, efficient, and cost-effective.

Some potential future applications of birdcall detection systems include:

Conservation: Birdcall detection systems can be used for monitoring endangered species, understanding their behavior and habitat, and implementing conservation strategies.

Agriculture: Birdcall detection systems can be used in agriculture to monitor bird populations, prevent crop damage, and manage pest control.

Bioacoustics research: Birdcall detection systems can aid in bioacoustics research to better understand the communication and behavior of birds.

Tourism: Birdcall detection systems can be used to enhance the bird watching experience for tourists by helping them identify different bird species and their calls.

Urban planning: Birdcall detection systems can be used in urban planning to monitor bird populations and inform decisions related to land use and development.

Overall, the future of birdcall detection systems is bright, and there is a lot of potential for these systems to have a positive impact on various fields, from conservation to agriculture to tourism.
